Needfinding for Strava

For this project, I explored how users interact with Strava, a fitness tracking platform that blends social media with performance analytics. Through interviews with three users—a former student-athlete, a casual exerciser, and an older cycling enthusiast—I investigated the tensions between motivation, privacy, and self-comparison that emerge when fitness gets intertwined with social media.

Strava is great at integrating data from GPS devices, phones, and smartwatches, offering real-time cues, nteractive maps, and providing options for peer feedback (like giving Kudos— essentially “likes”). But interviewees revealed key areas for improvement: default privacy settings, data accuracy, and the pressure to perform for an audience. While some users thrive on leaderboards and challenges, others feel overwhelmed or exposed, especially when tracking personal progress post-competition or training solo in remote areas.

Participants proposed thoughtful additions: AI-powered ride reviews, pace-hiding defaults, goal-based route planning, and AllTrails-style trail feedback. Overall, the project revealed the need for greater user control, optional social visibility, and customizable insights using AI to ensure Strava supports all types of users.
